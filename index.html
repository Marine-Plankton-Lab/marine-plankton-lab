Marine Biological Oceanography

Unraveling Hidden Life in the Ocean

Marine Plankton Lab focuses on the diversity, ecological function, and biological interactions of marine microorganisms and protists — particularly parasitic protists infecting dinoflagellates and diatoms, and mixotrophic dinoflagellates that occupy critical positions in the marine microbial food web.

Who We Are

Marine Plankton Lab at Pukyong National University's Department of Oceanography conducts in-depth research in biological oceanography — specifically the diversity, ecological roles, and molecular biology of marine microorganisms and protists.

Our research targets parasitic protists found in Korean coastal waters, including novel parasitic nanoflagellates, and examines how these parasites infect dinoflagellates and diatoms, influence dissolved organic carbon (DOC) production, and regulate harmful algal blooms. We integrate metagenomics, molecular phylogenetics, and microscopy to reveal infection mechanisms and energy cycling roles of these cryptic organisms.

Research Areas

What We Study

Three core research pillars drive our investigative agenda in marine microbial ecology and biological oceanography

Research Area 01
🦠
Diversity & Ecological Functions of Marine Parasitic Protists

We investigate the diversity and ecological roles of parasitic protists — especially those infecting dinoflagellates and diatoms in Korean coastal waters. We have characterized newly discovered parasitic nanoflagellates (Amoebophrya, Euduboscquella, Pirsonia, Pseudopirsonia) via morphology and molecular phylogenetics. We analyze how parasites regulate HAB species such as Cochlodinium polykrikoides and Akashiwo sanguinea, and how infection drives dissolved organic carbon (DOC) production and restructures marine microbial communities. Methods include metagenomics, qPCR, single-cell PCR, phylogenetics, and electron microscopy.

Research Area 02
Protist Community Structure in the Southeastern Sea of Korea

We conduct systematic surveys of protist community composition and seasonal dynamics in the southeastern sea of Korea — including the South Sea and Korea Strait — using both morphological identification and molecular approaches. Our research documents the spatial and temporal distribution of phototrophic, heterotrophic, and mixotrophic protists (dinoflagellates, ciliates, diatoms, and nanoflagellates), revealing how oceanographic conditions (water temperature, salinity, nutrients, currents) shape community structure. These data form a critical baseline for understanding ecosystem change and the spread of harmful species in Korean coastal waters.

Protist diversity Community structure Seasonal dynamics Korea Strait Diatom 18S rRNA eDNA
Research Area 03
🚨
Harmful Algal Blooms (HABs) — Detection, Ecology & Biocontrol

Harmful algal blooms (HABs) pose major threats to marine ecosystems and aquaculture industries along the Korean coast. We study the ecology, toxin production, and bloom dynamics of key HAB-forming species — including Cochlodinium polykrikoides, Alexandrium spp., Karenia spp., and Dinophysis spp. Using quantitative PCR (qPCR), multiplex detection assays, and field monitoring, we develop rapid molecular tools for early warning and species discrimination. We also investigate the role of parasitic protists (e.g., Amoebophrya) as natural biocontrol agents that terminate bloom events, and how environmental factors drive bloom initiation and collapse.
